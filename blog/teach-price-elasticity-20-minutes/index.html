How to Teach Price Elasticity of Demand in 20 Minutes — A Sixth Form Activity

Published 6 May 2026 · 8 min read · Lesson plan for Edexcel 1.3.3 & AQA 3.2.3

Most Sixth Form students can recite the price elasticity of demand formula within five minutes of meeting it. Asking them to use it a week later is a different story. Here's a 20-minute activity that actually makes the concept stick — no prior setup, no printed worksheets unless you want them.

You can run this as a standalone mini-lesson, a starter before a longer Theme 1 unit, or as exam revision. It works equally well for Edexcel A-Level Business (Theme 1.3.3), AQA 7132 (section 3.2.3), and OCR H431. It works even better if you follow it up with a simulation where they see elasticity in action — more on that at the end.

Why price elasticity is notoriously hard to teach

Three reasons, in my experience:

  1. The formula looks simple. Students write down "%ΔQd / %ΔP", get a negative number, and think they understand it. They don't — they've memorised a procedure.
  2. The answer is always "it depends". A-Level evaluation depends on the context of elasticity (substitutes, necessity, income share, time horizon). If all you teach is the number, students can't evaluate.
  3. The intuition is counter-intuitive. Students instinctively think "higher price = less demand, always". They don't initially grasp that for inelastic goods, price rises barely shift demand at all.

This activity attacks all three. It starts with a real-world paradox, moves to the formula as a tool to resolve it, and ends with a transferable exam instinct.

The 20-minute activity — step by step

Step 1 — The hook (3 min)

Project two images side by side: a petrol pump and a cinema ticket. Tell students:

"Petrol prices go up 20% next Monday. Cinema tickets also go up 20% next Monday. Which business will lose more customers?"

Let two or three students answer without you intervening. You will usually get the correct intuition — cinemas lose more — but students will struggle to explain why. Good. That's the gap the lesson fills.

Step 2 — Formalise the concept (3 min)

Introduce the formula on the board:

PED = % change in Qd ÷ % change in P

Work one calculation using the cinema example. If a 20% price rise causes a 30% drop in cinema attendance, PED = –30% / 20% = –1.5.

Key teaching point: the sign is almost always negative (the law of demand), so when examiners and textbooks say "elastic" or "inelastic" they're talking about the absolute value. |PED| > 1 = elastic. |PED| < 1 = inelastic. |PED| = 1 = unit-elastic.

Scaffolding tip: For mixed-ability groups, write the three categories as a number line on the board — from 0 (perfectly inelastic) to infinity (perfectly elastic) — and have students place the cinema example on it. Visual anchors help.

Step 3 — Team application (8 min)

Split the class into teams of 3–4. Give each team the following four scenarios (on slides, printed, or dictated):

The four scenarios

  1. A luxury Swiss watch brand raises prices by 15%.
  2. A supermarket own-brand loaf of bread goes up by 10p.
  3. A prescription insulin increases in price (assume a private-pay market for argument's sake).
  4. A Netflix subscription goes from £10.99 to £13.99 per month.

Each team answers three things for each scenario:

  • Is demand likely elastic, unit-elastic, or inelastic?
  • Why — in one sentence, referencing substitutes, necessity, income share, or time?
  • What would the business's total revenue do — rise, fall, or stay the same?
Classroom reality check: The bread scenario always causes argument. Some students say inelastic (necessity), others say elastic (cheap substitutes exist — other supermarkets, own-brand alternatives). Both can be right depending on whether you're the shop or the category. Use this to introduce cross elasticity and substitute goods — a natural bridge to the next sub-topic.

Step 4 — Reflect and link to exam language (6 min)

Bring the class back together. Reveal the expected answers but also validate good reasoning that arrived at a different conclusion. Then ask the exam-level question:

"You are advising a UK manufacturer through a recession. Would you rather sell a highly elastic product or a highly inelastic product? Explain your reasoning in 60 seconds."

This is the evaluation step. There is no single correct answer — that's the point. Inelastic products protect revenue (basic foods, utilities) but may lack growth potential. Elastic products lose badly in recessions but have upside during booms.

Finish by reading out the exam-style language students need to use:

  • "The degree of elasticity depends on…"
  • "In the short run… but in the long run…"
  • "This is particularly significant for a business because…"

Those three sentence starters are worth three evaluation marks each in the exam. Tell them so.

Common misconceptions to address

  • "Elastic means stretchy, so the price is stretchy." No. The price changes by whatever the business sets it to. What's "stretchy" is the quantity demanded in response.
  • "A negative PED means demand is falling." No. The minus sign just reflects the law of demand — as price rises, quantity falls. A PED of –2 doesn't mean demand is falling twice as fast; it means it's twice as responsive to a price change as the price change itself.
  • "Luxury goods are always elastic." Mostly, but not always. Veblen goods (Rolex, for example) can have positive PED in certain conditions — higher price signals status, demand rises. Worth mentioning to stretch A* students.
  • "If PED is inelastic, you should always raise prices." In theory, yes, revenue rises. In practice, there are brand, ethical, regulatory (think insulin, utilities) and long-run demand effects. Evaluation lives here.

Extension questions for stretch

  1. "If PED is –2.5, and the firm raises price by 4%, what happens to revenue?" (Answer: revenue falls, because quantity demanded falls by 10%.)
  2. "Why would PED for a holiday booked 6 months ahead be different from PED for the same holiday booked the night before?" (Time horizon, alternative options.)
  3. "Why would supermarkets deliberately price some items below cost (loss leaders) if demand for those items is relatively inelastic?" (Cross-elasticity, basket building, customer lock-in.)

Running the follow-up — a simulation that locks it in

The activity above teaches PED as a concept. The piece that students usually never get in a classroom is the feeling of setting a price too high and watching demand evaporate. That's where a simulation earns its keep.

In The Business School, students run their own virtual firm and set prices across multiple rounds. In round one they almost always over-price — they don't believe the market will punish them. Round two, after demand has collapsed, they correct. By round four they're instinctively asking: "What's the elasticity of this product?" before they move the slider.

If you're teaching Theme 1 or AQA section 3.2, pairing the 20-minute activity above with a 45-minute simulation in the same week covers the entire pricing sub-topic with depth a worksheet alone cannot.

Teacher FAQ

Can I run this with Year 12 right after they meet the demand curve?

Yes — this is the natural second lesson after basic demand. Make sure they are comfortable with "demand shifts" vs "movements along the curve" first, otherwise the cinema hook misfires.

Can I extend this to price elasticity of supply (PES)?

Yes, with a 10-minute add-on. Re-run the hook: "Oil platforms can't increase output overnight. Ice cream vans can hire extra staff tomorrow. Which supply is more responsive?" Same structure, different variable.

Does this work online / for home learners?

Yes. The four scenarios work beautifully as a shared Google Doc with each team writing in a different colour. The exam-style reflection works as a written response rather than spoken.

What worksheet should I use?

Honestly, none. The reason we built this as a 20-minute verbal activity is that worksheets for PED tend to reduce it to procedural calculation, which is the opposite of what A-Level examiners want. If you need a take-home, the three extension questions above work as 15 minutes of homework.
