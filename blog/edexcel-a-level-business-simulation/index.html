Why a simulation, and why this one

The Edexcel A-Level Business specification is strong on decision-making language — "evaluate the impact of…", "analyse the likely effect on…", "to what extent…". Pupils can memorise the terminology. What they can't easily memorise is what it feels like to set a price too high and watch sales collapse, or what happens when you carry too much stock into a slow month.

That's what simulations do well. A good one forces pupils to make decisions under time pressure, lets them see the consequences in the same lesson, and gives them something concrete to write about in the exam six months later. The bad simulations either over-gamify it (students compete, nobody learns) or under-gamify it (students fill in a spreadsheet and want to leave).

The Business School is built specifically for A-Level and Sixth Form classrooms. Pupils form teams of up to five, each person takes a C-suite role (CEO, CFO, CMO, COO, CTO), and over eight in-game rounds they react to a real market — pricing, stocking, marketing spend, hiring. The 60-minute lesson plan

This assumes 28–30 pupils and a reliable projector. You need the demo URL, a class set of devices (Chromebooks, laptops, or phones all work), and a whiteboard.

0–5 min · Setup and hook

Project the teacher dashboard. Ask pupils: "If I gave you £10,000 and told you to run a business for an hour, what would you do first?" Let three answer. Then share the game PIN — pupils join via browser, pick a team name, and get to the lobby.

Pro tip: pre-assign teams. Random self-selection wastes 4 minutes.

5–10 min · Roles and objective

Each team assigns C-suite roles. Explain objective: at the end of 8 rounds, the team with the highest combined net assets wins. Not revenue. Net assets. Write it on the board. Ask: "Why might we measure net assets, not revenue?" — you've just opened the profit vs. turnover distinction that examiners love.

10–45 min · Play rounds 1–6

Each round is ~4–5 minutes. Teams make decisions, the market simulates, they see results. Your job on the dashboard:

  • Round 2: inject a mild demand shift — teams with rigid pricing strategies start to feel it.
  • Round 4: drop a "raw material price up 20%" crisis. Watch teams react differently.
  • Round 5: pause. Ask one team to explain their current strategy to the class in 60 seconds. It forces articulation.
Classroom reality check: Rounds 1 and 2 are always chaos. Don't fix it. Chaos round one is learning. By round 3 the sensible teams have self-regulated.

45–55 min · Play rounds 7–8 and reveal

Let the final rounds play out. Leaderboard projects the result. Don't do a long prize ceremony — 20 seconds is plenty.

55–60 min · Reflection (the part that makes this a lesson, not a game)

This is non-negotiable. Pick three of these and run them as whole-class discussion:

  1. "Which team changed strategy mid-game? What triggered it? Link this to strategic drift (Theme 3).".
  2. "The winning team — was it because of a good decision, or because a competitor's decision was bad? How would you know in a real business?"
  3. "If the exchange rate crisis hit in round 4 — which team is more exposed, the one with overseas suppliers or the one with domestic? (Theme 4.1)"
  4. "Who had the best cash position in round 3 but didn't win? What does that tell us about cash vs. profit?"
  5. "If we ran this 10 times, would the same team always win? What does that tell us about luck vs. strategy in real business?"

Homework: one paragraph — "Using the simulation and one theorist, explain why our team [won / lost / stayed mid-table]." Link it to a 2-mark exam question they've already seen.

What actually works — and what doesn't

What works

  • Pre-assigned teams. Mixed-ability, 4–5 per team. Random is a waste.
  • One crisis per half. Don't spam crises — pupils stop engaging if every round is on fire.
  • The reflection 5 minutes. Without it this is a game. With it it's a lesson.
  • Running it twice. The second time the same year group plays, they're strategic. Compare the two sessions — that's A-Level gold.

What doesn't

  • Long intros. Keep the setup under 10 min or you'll lose rounds at the end.
  • Marking the simulation itself. Don't. Mark the written reflection instead — otherwise weaker pupils feel punished for losing.
  • Running it as cover. It needs you on the dashboard. A cover teacher can't drop crises at the right moment.

For teachers — what you need to prep
