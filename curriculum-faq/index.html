Read this first — what the simulation is for

The Business School is an application and consolidation tool, not a content-delivery tool. You teach the specification; the simulation gives students a live context to apply it under time pressure, then reflect on the trade-offs. It is at its most powerful as a consolidation lesson after a theme, or as mock-prep before a Section B / extended-response assessment. It complements your teaching — it does not replace it.

1 · Specification coverage

Which boards, which themes, and how directly the in-game mechanics exercise each one.

Which exam specifications does the simulation map to?

The simulation is built around the decision-making content shared across UK Business specifications — pricing, finance, operations, external influences and strategy. It maps directly to:

  • Edexcel A-Level Business 9BS0 — Themes 1–4
  • AQA A-Level Business 7132 — sections 3.2–3.10
  • OCR A-Level Business H431 — marketing, operations, finance and strategy units

It also supports Pearson BTEC Level 3 Business (applied decision-making units), Cambridge IGCSE Business Studies 0450 and IB Business Management. A full sub-clause-by-sub-clause breakdown with coverage levels lives on the Curriculum Mapping page.

Which AQA 7132 sections does it cover?

Most directly 3.3 (improving marketing performance) and 3.5 (improving financial performance) — both fully exercised by the core mechanics. The simulation also supports:

  • 3.2 Managers, leadership and decision making — the strategy choice and decisions under time pressure
  • 3.4 Improving operational performance — stock and supply-chain response under crisis events
  • 3.7–3.10 Strategic position, direction, methods and managing change — the live leaderboard, strategic pivots and crisis-response loop

It is a strong fit for revisiting these sections as applied practice once the underlying theory has been taught.

Does it work for OCR H431, BTEC, IGCSE or IB?

OCR H431 — yes. The marketing, operations, finance and strategy content maps cleanly; the in-game mechanics are board-agnostic at the decision-making level.

BTEC Level 3 — strong fit for applied, scenario-based units where students make and justify business decisions.

Cambridge IGCSE 0450 — works as an introduction to business activity, finance and marketing for KS4 / lower sixth.

IB Business Management — supports the marketing, finance and operations toolkits and the emphasis on decision-making in context.

The underlying skill — apply a concept, see the consequence, justify the next decision — is common to all of them.

2 · Assessment objectives & exam skills

How time in the simulation converts into marks on the page.

How does playing actually translate into exam marks?

The simulation is built to develop all four assessment objectives, with its real strength in the higher bands:

  • AO1 (Knowledge) — students recall and use terminology (price elasticity, contribution, working capital, VAT) at the moment of decision.
  • AO2 (Application) — every pricing, stocking and contract decision is theory applied to the firm's specific context. Strong.
  • AO3 (Analysis) — cause-and-effect surfaces live: a price rise produces a measurable demand drop; a stock-out a measurable margin hit. Students watch the analysis chain happen. Strong.
  • AO4 (Evaluation) — the end-of-session reflection and auto-generated report give students concrete trade-offs to weigh in their next written answer — the "in the short run … but in the long run …" construction examiners reward. Strong.

The mechanism is simple: an extended-response answer written after a student has lived a Supply Crisis carries concrete cause-effect chains and balanced evaluation, rather than generic textbook recall.

Does it help specifically with the 16 / 20 / 25-mark evaluation questions?

Yes — that is its sharpest use. The highest-tariff extended-response questions are won on AO3 analysis and AO4 evaluation, not on AO1 knowledge. Students lose marks when their analysis is generic and their judgement is unsupported.

Because the simulation makes students live the trade-off — absorb the cost and lose margin, or pass it on and lose market share — they walk into the written task with a concrete, contextual example to anchor both the analysis and the "it depends on…" judgement. Pair it with our free Model Answers and Sentence Stem Library to convert the experience into exam phrasing.

Is there evidence of impact?

Honestly: the Autumn 2026 Pilot Programme (15 September – 15 December 2026) is gathering that evidence now, across a cohort of UK and international schools. We will publish anonymised findings in December 2026.

What we can show today is the pedagogical mechanism — applied decision-making plus immediate cause-effect feedback plus structured reflection — which is well-established in business-education research as a driver of higher-order thinking. 3 · In the classroom

What students actually do, and where it fits your scheme of work.

What do students actually do in a session?

Each student (or team) runs a competing firm and works through live decision modules. Every module maps to specification content:

In-game moduleSpecification content exercised
Marketplace & Launch ProductPricing, product mix, promotion — marketing decision-making
Capital & LoansSources of finance, gearing, interest and repayment
VAT / HMRC PortalCash flow, working capital, statutory compliance
Hiring AuctionHuman resources, recruitment and labour as a cost
InvestmentsRisk vs return — property yield vs live equity prices
Crisis ResponseExternal influences — recession, supply shock, tax change
ESG dilemmas (class vote)Ethics, stakeholders and corporate culture

The teacher controls the pace from a dashboard, triggers crisis events, and projects a live leaderboard. Sessions close with student reflection and an auto-generated class report.

Where does it fit in my scheme of work?

Two reliable entry points:

  • Mid-theme consolidation — after teaching pricing, finance or external influences, run the simulation as a single lesson and reflect against the content just covered.
  • Mock-prep week — run it 5–7 days before a Section B / extended-response mock. Students reference the lived experience directly in their evaluation paragraphs.

It is flexible-length: the teacher controls phase timers, so a session runs anywhere from a 15-minute tutor slot to a full 60-minute deep dive.

How many students, and on what devices?

Up to 30 students per session, as individuals or in teams. Any modern browser works — Chromebook, laptop, tablet or phone — and the teacher projects the dashboard from their own laptop. No app to install. Larger classes split across two parallel sessions with different PINs.
